The cross-line optimization should optimize at the plant level, not just the individual line level. As a core objective, aim to distribute workload such that all eligible production lines are utilized as evenly as practicable, avoiding situations where some lines are heavily loaded while others remain significantly underutilized while other eligible lines have available capacity.

When assigning or reassigning orders across lines, the optimizer should jointly consider overall capacity balancing, changeover reduction, die change minimization, sequencing efficiency, and profitability. The objective is to maximize total plant throughput by intelligently utilizing available capacity across all eligible lines rather than optimizing each line in isolation.

Orders may be reassigned to another feedmill line only if that SKU/product is eligible and permitted to be processed on that line according to the existing line capability matrix and business rules. The optimizer should proactively evaluate all eligible lines for each order instead of defaulting to its current assignment, selecting the assignment that provides the best overall plant-wide outcome. Under no circumstance should an order be assigned to a line that is not authorized or technically capable of producing that SKU/product.

In addition, the optimizer should actively minimize the frequency of die (diameter) changes whenever operationally feasible. Since die changes incur significant setup time and are operationally undesirable, the scheduler should prefer longer continuous production runs of the same diameter and avoid unnecessary switching between diameters. Rather than merely accounting for the 1.00-hour die change penalty, the optimizer should seek to avoid creating the die change in the first place by intelligently grouping compatible orders or assigning them to another eligible feedmill line if doing so results in fewer overall die changes and a better plant-wide schedule.

This optimization should be evaluated from a global plant perspective. A solution that slightly increases changeovers on one line but substantially reduces total die changes and improves capacity balance across the entire plant may be preferable to optimizing each line independently. The optimizer should therefore seek the best overall plant objective, considering total changeover hours, die continuity, balanced utilization, throughput, and scheduling efficiency together.

These objectives are soft optimization objectives, not hard constraints. They must never violate or compromise existing business rules, including product eligibility, line compatibility, MTO deadline protection, Critical/Urgent/Monitor handling, mash scheduling rules, production dependencies, completion-feasibility requirements, date protections, capacity limits, or any other established scheduling logic. If a more balanced allocation or fewer die changes would require breaking these rules, the existing hard constraints must always take precedence.

The goal is to produce the most operationally efficient plant-wide schedule by balancing line utilization, maximizing continuous runs of compatible products, minimizing unnecessary die changes and other changeovers, and utilizing all eligible feedmill lines effectively, while fully preserving the existing scheduling safeguards and business rules.
