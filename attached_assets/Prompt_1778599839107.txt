Please refine the AI date suggestion logic so that the order’s projected **Estimated Completion Date/Time** is used as an important planning guide and validation signal — but not as an absolute hard constraint by itself.

## IMPORTANT CORRECTION

Do not treat Estimated Completion Date/Time as a strict hard blocker in all cases.

In the preview, Estimated Completion is often derived from:
- the current first-order start anchor
- default start date/time (such as today at 8:00 AM)
- production hours
- cascading sequence logic

Because of that, some later-dated orders (for example avail dates on May 20, May 22, May 23) may still show much earlier Estimated Completion dates in the preview, simply because the cascade starts early.

That does **not** automatically mean the suggestion is wrong.

So Estimated Completion should be used as:
- a guide
- a feasibility signal
- a lateness detection input
- a reasoning input

but not as a universal hard rejection rule.

---

## REQUIRED COMPARISON LOGIC

The AI should evaluate the relationship between:

- Start Date / Start Time
- Estimated Completion Date/Time
- Avail Date

using the planning logic:

### Avail Date >= Estimated Completion Date >= Start Date/Time

This should be interpreted as a target planning relationship, not an absolute rigid equality rule.

---

## WHAT THE AI SHOULD ASK

For each order, the AI should evaluate:

### 1. Is the Avail Date later than or equal to the Estimated Completion Date?
If yes:
- that is generally acceptable
- the order is not late based on this signal
- no forced correction is needed just because the dates differ

### 2. Is the Estimated Completion Date later than the Avail Date?
If yes:
- this is a strong lateness / delay signal
- the AI should consider:
  - advancing the order earlier
  - improving the suggested start date/time
  - changing sequence position
  - explaining the lateness risk in Sequence Insight

This is especially important for long-running orders.

---

## EXAMPLE OF CORRECT INTERPRETATION

Example:
- Order A = Avail Date May 12 = Completion May 12 12:19 PM
- Order B = Avail Date May 13 = Completion May 13 4:18 AM
- Order C = Avail Date May 22 = Completion May 13 5:48 AM
- Order D = Avail Date May 23 = Completion May 13 7:53 AM

Interpretation:
- Order A → okay if completion is within May 12
- Order B → acceptable if completion still aligns before or within its due window
- Order C → not a problem just because completion is much earlier than May 22
- Order D → not a problem just because completion is much earlier than May 23

So the AI should not reject Orders C and D based on Estimated Completion being earlier than Avail Date.

The real problem case is when:
- Estimated Completion > Avail Date

That is what the AI should treat as a warning signal.

---

## USE ESTIMATED COMPLETION AS A DELAY DETECTION SIGNAL

If a candidate order has:
- Avail Date = May 12
- Estimated Completion = May 13 4:18 AM

then this is a likely delay signal.

The AI should not blindly keep the same suggested date.
Instead, it should:
- treat this as evidence that the order may need earlier placement
- or a better anchor
- or better sequencing

But still interpret it in context of:
- the current sequence anchor
- user overrides
- strategy intent
- the fact that preview completion is derived and may change if sequence changes

---

## DO NOT FORCE ALL DATES TO MATCH THE CURRENT CASCADE

Avoid making the AI overly rigid by locking date suggestions to the currently shown Estimated Completion cascade.

The AI should understand that:
- Estimated Completion is influenced by the current preview anchor
- sequence changes can shift completion
- moving the order or changing anchor can improve the result

So use Estimated Completion as a strong evaluation signal, not as an immutable truth.

---

## SEQUENCE INSIGHT SHOULD EXPLAIN WHEN THIS SIGNAL MATTERS

If the AI detects:
- Estimated Completion > Avail Date

then Sequence Insight should explain that.

Example:
“This order was advanced because, under the current sequence timing, its projected completion would exceed its avail date, indicating likely delay risk.”

If:
- Estimated Completion < Avail Date

then no warning is needed simply because the order completes earlier than required.
